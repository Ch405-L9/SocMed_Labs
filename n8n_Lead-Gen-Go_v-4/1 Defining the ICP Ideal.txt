4. Enrichment (Safe Crawling and Contact Mining)  
For each candidate, fetch its website (if we have a URL from OSM or can construct it from the name/domain). Use a polite HTTP client (Python requests or httpx) with: a realistic user-agent string (identifying as a friendly crawler), respect for robots.txt, and rate-limiting (e.g. 1–2 requests/second)
. First, read https://<domain>/robots.txt and skip any disallowed paths. Always obey robots.txt directives (disallowed paths) even if not legally binding
. This is both ethical and reduces block risk.

When fetching, record: HTTP status code, whether HTTPS was used (preferring https://), and load time. Parse the HTML (e.g. with BeautifulSoup or html5lib). Extract:

Emails: Look for mailto: links and run a regex search for email patterns. Alternatively, use an OSINT tool like theHarvester (open-source) which auto-queries public sources (search engines, etc.) for email addresses and names from a domain

Phone/Social: Extract phone numbers from HTML text or images (regex for phone patterns). Many small business sites have a contact page listing phones and social media.

Best Contact Person: If available, identify decision-makers. Look for “About Us” or “Team” pages; scan for titles like “Dr.” or “Attorney” followed by names. A local NLP/LLM could help parse “Our team includes Dr. Smith (CEO)” or “Contact: John Doe, Managing Attorney”. (This can be done later or manually if needed.)

Business Hours: First, check OSM data for an opening_hours tag (if present). If not, attempt to scrape hours from the website (look for strings like “Mon-Fri”, “Open”, etc.). Many sites list hours on the contact page or footer; a targeted regex or keyword search (“Open”, “Hours”, “Monday”) can extract approximate schedule. Store any hours found.

Store all new fields (primary email, all emails, site load time, HTTP status, has HTTPS?, opening_hours, etc.) into an enriched leads CSV.

5. Lead Scoring & Prioritization 
Assign simple rule-based scores to prioritize leads. For example:

Fit score: +points if in the target industry (we already filtered for doctors/law), +more if business size seems right (e.g. name implies a small practice). We might also score higher if their site shows signs of outdated tech (no HTTPS, old CMS), since “patch data leaks with webops” is our service – a vulnerable-looking site increases urgency.

Reachability score: +points for each valid contact found (email, phone). E.g. having a direct email or multiple addresses yields a high reach score.

Urgency score: +points if site is insecure (no HTTPS) or missing visible privacy notices (indicating likely compliance gaps).

Combine into a total score. (Using a local LLM at this stage is optional: one could prompt it to evaluate “How tech-savvy does this site look?” or “Is this business likely to need web security?”, but simple heuristics usually suffice.)

Then sort the enriched list by total score. The goal is to produce 20 leads per day, of which the top ~5 are “highly likely” contacts. These top leads (with full contact info, best contact person, hours, etc.) go to outreach. Lower-scored leads can be deferred or re-evaluated later.
